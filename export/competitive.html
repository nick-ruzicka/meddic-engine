Competitive signals should find reps before deals get hurt.

Today, competitive intelligence at most enterprise teams is reactive. A rep hears a competitor mentioned on a Gong call and learns that their deal is at risk after the fact. This system inverts that — it watches public surfaces where competitors telegraph what they're building, synthesizes the signal, and routes it to the reps who need to know before the call happens. The thesis tested here is simple: public signals, read consistently and scored correctly, predict deal risk earlier than reactive feedback alone. This page documents what we monitor, how we route signal, and whether the hypothesis holds.

What this is a piece of

The competitive intelligence tool is one surface. The thesis is bigger.

Every rep in institutional sales lives with the same problem: they own a book of 20-80 accounts, each with deep context — buyer history, competitive dynamics, product usage, deal stage, recent signals — and no single system holds all of it in one place. CRM has some. Gong has some. Slack has some. The rep's head holds the rest, fragmented across conversations and pulled up from memory on the call.

The end state being built toward is a GTM second brain per rep: full territory context — buyers, signals, competitive intel, deal history, product usage — available wherever the rep already works, through MCP. Every rep walks into every call with the intelligence of their best day, not their average day. Competitive intelligence is one layer of that brain. The three-layer architecture — Distribution, Action, Intelligence — documented below is how that brain reaches the rep. This page documents the first working layer.

What this system doesn't see

No serious competitive intelligence is omniscient.

Being clear about the gaps is how the system stays trustworthy. These are the things the pipeline deliberately does not and cannot see:

  • Private product betas before public announcement
  • Internal strategy memos, sales training, roadmap documents
  • Unannounced customer wins and losses (unless the customer announces them)
  • Content behind login walls — most AlphaSense product docs are gated, for example
  • Conference booth conversations, analyst briefings, off-record chatter
  • Intent signal from reps on calls — that's the other half of the loop (see below)

System design

How every rep would get rich competitive intel they can't ignore

Everything in this section is architecture, not current state. See status section below for what's live today.

Surfacing the signal is the easy half. Getting it to the right rep at the right moment without overwhelming them is what turns competitive intelligence from a research exercise into an operating system. The design below extends the signal engine you've already seen into a three-layer architecture — Distribution, Action, Intelligence. What's live today is in the status section at the bottom of this page. Everything else is the design thesis being tested.

Distribution

DESIGNED · NOT YET SHIPPED
  • Morning brief in the rep's Slack when a tier-1 competitor ships or announces — filtered to only the accounts in that rep's territory that are likely affected
  • CRM-synced email summary weekly — competitive delta for every open opportunity
  • Ambient alerts for deal-risk signals — when a competitor signal pairs with a Gong mention on the same account, the system fires a direct DM
  • Distribution doesn't stop at the rep. Same signal pipeline, different audiences — reps get territory-filtered alerts, managers get team digest, leadership gets aggregate patterns, product gets competitor-move clustering. Single-rep distribution is single-point-of-failure by design; multi-audience distribution is how organizational memory forms.

Action

DESIGNED · NOT YET SHIPPED
  • Auto-drafted rebuttal language for the specific competitive claim — tied to the source document the competitor published
  • CRM fields updated on the account — competitive context written into Salesforce for every other agent (CSM playbooks, renewal plays) to read
  • Signal-specific talk tracks generated and pushed to Gong — the rep walks into the next call with the exact counter-positioning

Intelligence

PARTIALLY LIVE
  • Weekly brief regeneration from accumulated signals
  • Gong feedback loop — when reps confirm they heard a competitor on a call, the system weights that competitor's signal higher
  • Deal outcome feedback — closed-won and closed-lost pairs get analyzed; competitive signals that predicted deal risk get scored up, noise signals get scored down

The point isn't to send reps more alerts. It's to send them the three things they can't afford to miss — routed to the territory they actually own.

Context at scale

Why the second brain doesn't collapse under its own weight.

A naive version of this system blows up immediately. If every rep's Claude instance holds the full company's signal library, the token cost is prohibitive and the relevance drops to zero. The architecture has to be rep-scoped, not company-scoped.

Three design principles:

  • Territory is the primary filter. Context loaded into Claude for any given rep is scoped to the accounts in their book, plus a small buffer of expansion targets. A rep working 25 large asset-manager accounts doesn't get loaded with 300 PE accounts — those signals exist but don't enter their context. Same pipeline, different slice per rep.
  • Hierarchical memory, not flat memory. Every account has a tiered memory: (1) always-on facts — buyer names, deal stage, last touched, competitive context; (2) recent-on facts — signals and activities from the last 14 days; (3) archive — older context, retrieved only when the rep asks for it or a specific signal pattern triggers it. Claude gets tier 1 + 2 by default, tier 3 on demand. This is the difference between a system that costs $5/rep/month and one that costs $500.
  • MCP as the retrieval layer, not the context layer. Rather than loading everything into context, expose it as MCP servers — one per domain (CRM, competitive intel, product telemetry, Gong summaries). Claude pulls what it needs, when it needs it, through tool calls. The rep's context stays lean; the memory stays rich. This is how you get a second brain without a trillion-token context window.

None of this is live today. The current system runs one rep (me) with tier-1 context only. Tier 2 and 3 memory, territory-scoped loading, and MCP retrieval are the Day 60 work.

30-60-90 roadmap

This is the plan I'd execute against if I came in for Day 1.

Day 30 — Signal engine live, one AE pod.

The competitive intelligence tool you're looking at now, expanded from 5 to 15 competitors, plus the core GTM signal engine extended to the platform's ICP. Three AEs opted in to a daily territory-filtered Slack brief. Instrumented from Day 1 — approval rates, open rates, response rates logged every day.

Not yet: manager/leadership distribution, CRM writeback, any autonomy.

Day 60 — Action layer + memory architecture.

Signals route beyond the rep: team digest to managers, aggregate pattern report to leadership. CRM writeback lands — every signal becomes a structured Salesforce field other agents can read. Outreach push on approve. Gong pairing (reactive + proactive signals combined on the same account). Memory architecture: hierarchical tiering so context stays lean and rich. Pod expanded to 8 AEs.

Not yet: MCP retrieval, autonomous CSM actions, product telemetry integration.

Day 90 — Second brain, early form.

MCP retrieval across three domains (competitive intel, CRM/deal context, product telemetry). Every AE in pilot cohort has a Claude interface that answers “what's happening in my book this week?” with full rep-scoped context. Tier 3 archive memory queryable on demand. CSM playbook autonomy for structural signals (DAU drop, seat activation stall). First outcome data: 90-day close-rate comparison on contested deals, AE-by-AE.

Not yet: full GTM rollout, fully autonomous outbound (and by design — outbound stays human-in-the-loop).

Day 90 is not “finished GTM second brain for all of .” It's the working prototype, validated with data, that tells whether to invest in scaling it to the full GTM org. The goal is to answer the empirical question, not to ship the final product.

Does this actually work?

The whole system is an empirical question. Here's how we'd answer it.

The hypothesis: reps who receive this signal will close more deals in contested situations than reps who don't. That's testable. Here's how:

  1. Baseline. Measure close rate on deals where a competitor was mentioned on a Gong call, before the system ships.
  2. Instrument. Tag every opportunity where the system fires a competitive alert to a rep. Capture timestamp, signal type, and whether the rep acknowledged it.
  3. Compare. Over a 90-day window, compare close rate and cycle time between two cohorts: deals where reps received + engaged with a signal, and deals where they didn't.
  4. Iterate. If signals that predicted outcomes scored low in the relevance model, retrain. If signals that scored high produced no lift, retrain. Every closed deal is a labeled training example.

Some effects compound without being directly measurable. Reps who see competitive intelligence every morning know their market better than reps who don't — and that shows up in pitch quality, pricing conversations, and the willingness to push back in contested deals. You can't A/B test the education of a sales team over six months, but you can measure whether the instrumented team closes harder deals at higher ACVs. The empirical question is narrow. The compounding effects are broader.

The system isn't the answer. It's the infrastructure to run the experiment.
